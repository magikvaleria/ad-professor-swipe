template: |
  1. Name a group of people most would dismiss, misunderstand, or pity ("the crazy ones / the misfits / the rebels / the ones who can't sit still").
  2. Reframe their flaws as virtues using parallel grammatical structure — "the ones who X are the ones who Y."
  3. Stack social proof by showing icons your audience already reveres who belong to this same group.
  4. Withhold the product entirely. The brand appears only as a quiet signature at the end.
  5. The viewer self-selects into the group by recognizing themselves in the description — which is much stickier than being told what to feel.
appliedExample: |
  Two examples — one in a totally different category (to test that the template generalizes), one in the actual brand it's about to be used for (to test that it fits).

  **A — Different category: a small accounting firm targeting indie founders.**

  "Here's to the ones who do the books at 11pm. The ones with seven spreadsheets and one Stripe account. The ones who'd rather build the thing than file the receipts. They're often called disorganized. We call them busy making something. Because the ones crazy enough to start a company are the ones who shouldn't be doing their own taxes."

  [Firm logo, quiet, no tagline]

  **B — Same category, real brand: Nature's Shimmer Watercolor Workshop, $47.**

  "Here's to the ones with three unopened paint sets in a drawer. The ones who saved the gilded postcard and the illuminated-manuscript page on a board they'll never show anyone. The ones who picked the paint with the gold in it because it looked alive — and put it away, because grown-ups aren't supposed to want sparkle. The ones who say they're 'not the artistic one' before they've ever held a brush long enough to find out. They've been told their eye is decorative. We think their eye is doing the work."

  [A single sheet of cotton paper drying in afternoon light, one wash of gold pigment settling into the fiber. No logo, no price, no call to action — just the work.]

  Same template both times. The mechanism is identical: the reader recognizes herself in a generous reframe — *I do save those, I did pick the gold one* — before the brand is named, so the workshop arrives as permission rather than a pitch. Notice the withholding does even more work here than for Apple: a watercolor course is a low-stakes purchase that lives or dies on whether the buyer believes she's the kind of person who paints. The product is the permission slip.

A note on why this still works almost thirty years later: the ad sells nothing functional about a Macintosh. It sells a position — "if you think of yourself as the kind of person who'd rather change the world than follow the rules, Apple is your brand." Every iPhone purchase since has been partly a renewal of that membership.

The genius is the withholding. Most ads exhaust their argument before the logo. This one builds its case for sixty seconds and lets the viewer arrive at the brand on their own — which is a much stickier kind of belief than being told what to think.

What to steal: the structure of "reframe a dismissed group → stack proof → withhold the product." What to leave behind: the scale. You don't need Einstein. A small business can run this template with three customer faces and a kitchen-table voiceover.
