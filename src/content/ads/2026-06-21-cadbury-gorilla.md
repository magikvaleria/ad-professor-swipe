structure: |
  A 90-second narrative of pure anticipation in three acts. Act one is absorption (a gorilla, no music, no explanation, what is this). Act two is build (Phil Collins's "In the Air Tonight" begins, the gorilla settles, the audience remembers the song). Act three is release (the famous drum fill lands, the gorilla drums with abandon, the audience is inside the joy). Logo and tagline appear in the last five seconds. The chocolate bar appears for two seconds. Nothing is claimed.
template: |
  1. Decide the single emotion your brand actually delivers, beyond any feature.
  2. Reverse-engineer that emotion into a story object. A character, a moment, a song, a payoff. Something that creates the emotion in real time, not in description.
  3. Build the piece as an experience, not a pitch. Withhold the product entirely until the emotion has been delivered.
  4. Place the brand at the moment of emotional peak so the audience attributes the feeling to the brand without being told to.
  5. Trust the audience to make the connection. They will. The pleasure of having figured it out themselves doubles their attachment to the brand.
appliedExample: |
  Two examples. One in a different category to prove the template generalizes. One in the actual brand it is about to be used for to prove it fits.

  **A. Different category: a sleep company.**

  Most sleep ads sell the mattress. Foam density, materials, reviews, "wake up refreshed." The Gorilla move is to deliver the feeling of sleep, in real time, for ninety seconds.

  Ninety seconds of a hand pulling a heavy cotton duvet up to a chin in a dim, blue-lit room. The body relaxing in slow micro-movements. No voiceover. The only sound is breathing slowing down. At second 80 the breath becomes longer, longer, longer. At second 88 it is the breath of someone fully asleep. The brand mark appears for the last two seconds. *Made for this.*

  No claim about cortisol. No mattress shown. The viewer has just experienced sleep onset in their own nervous system. The brand absorbs it.

  **B. Same category, real brand: Nature's Shimmer Watercolor Workshop, $47.**

  Most watercolor course ads sell the techniques. How to mix. How to layer. Before/after. The Gorilla move is to deliver the feeling of watercolor for sixty seconds and let the workshop absorb it.

  Sixty seconds. A kitchen window in late afternoon. Warm light. A small white dish of water. A finger dips into the water, then into a fresh pan of gold-flecked pigment. The brush lifts, full and heavy. It touches paper. The pigment blooms. The shimmer settles. The brush dips again. The dish of water turns gold. No other action. No music until the final eight seconds, when a single piano note holds. The shot pulls back to show one finished page, drying in the light. The brand mark appears. *Shimmer Watercolor Studio.* Two seconds, then black.

  No price. No "you will learn." No call to action. The feeling is the entire pitch. The audience knows what they just felt, and they know exactly what product caused it.

  The translation is direct. The Cadbury move treats the audience as already wanting the experience, only needing to feel it once. Valerie's audience IS already wanting the experience. They fantasize about painting. The standard course ad describes the experience. This one delivers it.

A note on why this still works almost twenty years later: Gorilla broke a rule that everyone in advertising thought was inviolable. The rule was you sell the product. The ad sold no product. The bar of chocolate is on screen for two seconds at the end of ninety. By any rational metric the spot should have failed. Cadbury Dairy Milk sales lifted significantly in the months after it aired and the spot won the Grand Prix at Cannes the following year.

The genius is structural. The film does not say "Cadbury makes you feel joyful." It MAKES you feel joyful, and the only branded thing in the frame at the peak of that feeling is Cadbury. The brain attributes felt-states to whatever is salient at the moment they peak. The ad simply controls salience at the right moment.

What to steal: the discipline of refusing the claim. Before any creative brief, ask "what is the felt-state our product actually delivers, in the body, in three seconds of use." Then ask "what would a 60-second piece of film that DELIVERS that felt-state, in real time, look like." That second question is what most brands never bother to answer because they are too busy describing.

What to leave behind: the scale. Cadbury bought broadcast TV inventory across the UK. A small brand cannot. The good news is the felt-states do not require a million-pound production budget. A locked-off camera in afternoon light, real materials, real hands, and a soundtrack of silence will do. The mechanism is the production, not the production budget.
