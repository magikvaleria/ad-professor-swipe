template: |
  1. Identify the feeling your audience has when they are at their most idealized version of using your product. Not the feature. The feeling.
  2. Coin a phrase that names that feeling with permanence. The phrase should sound like it has been true for a hundred years, even if you just wrote it.
  3. Pair the phrase with the simplest possible visual. Silhouette. A single object. Negative space. The phrase has to do most of the work.
  4. Use the phrase in every medium, every season, every campaign, for at least a decade. Do not get clever. Do not refresh the line. The repetition is the meaning.
  5. Trust the culture to absorb the meaning. Once absorbed, the meaning becomes property of your brand even after your brand fades. The symbol survives the company.
appliedExample: |
  Two examples. One in a different category to prove the template generalizes. One in the actual brand it is about to be used for to prove it fits.

  **A. Different category: a private-journal brand.**

  Most stationery ads sell the paper, the leather, the design. The De Beers move is to coin a phrase that names the feeling of using the product.

  *Some pages are only yours.*

  A single photograph of a closed leather-bound journal on a windowsill. The phrase set underneath, italic serif, generous negative space. Body copy is two sentences in fine type: "Most pages are public now. Most thoughts are public now. We make a place for the ones that aren't."

  Brand mark at the bottom, small.

  The phrase becomes the brand. The phrase is the entire campaign for the next decade. Every season, every medium, every product variant: same phrase. Same line. The line is repeated until "some pages are only yours" is the language a customer reaches for when she describes the feeling of opening her own journal at the kitchen table on a Sunday.

  **B. Same category, real brand: Nature's Shimmer Watercolor Workshop.**

  The category-default for art courses is to sell skill. The De Beers move is to coin a phrase that names a feeling that attaches permanently to the act of painting.

  *Some afternoons are made of gold.*

  A single photograph of a kitchen table in late afternoon sun. A small dish of water turning gold from the pigment. A brush at rest beside a half-finished shimmer wash. The line is the entire ad. Two sentences in italic serif underneath: "Most afternoons disappear. A painting afternoon doesn't."

  Italic serif at the bottom, small: "Nature's Shimmer Watercolor Studio."

  No price. No CTA. The line is the symbol. Used in every social post for the next decade. Used as the closer of every email. Used on the supply-list PDF, the lesson workbook, the course platform header. Used as the only line on the studio's first physical postcard. By 2032, "some afternoons are made of gold" is the language a graduate of the workshop reaches for when she describes the feeling of having painted on a Sunday.

  The mechanism is identical. The line names a feeling the audience has already had but has not been given language for (the particular permanence of a painting afternoon, when ordinary time is overwritten by the act of paying attention to one page). Coined once, the brand owns the language. Anyone who has painted an afternoon and then had it survive in memory will reach for the line. The brand inherits every retrieval.

A note on why "A diamond is forever" outranks almost every other slogan in advertising history: a junior copywriter on a deadline wrote four words once, and those four words became the operative cultural belief about engagement for three generations. The line did not describe a fact. It manufactured one. Every subsequent diamond purchase since 1947 has been, at the cultural level, paying a small tax to Frances Gerety.

The discipline is patience. Most brands cannot run the same line for ten years let alone sixty-five. They get bored. They refresh. They lose the cumulative effect. The compounding only works if you do not break it.

What to steal: write the line. Not the seasonal tagline. The line. The one you commit to using in every email signature, every product page, every social post, every supply list PDF for ten years. If you do not have a line worth committing to for ten years, you do not have a line. Go back and write a better one.

What to leave behind: the cynical edge. De Beers' line worked partly because it has a touch of marketing manipulation in it (the line literally made the diamond market) and most modern audiences are allergic to that flavor. The 2026 version of this move has to be a line that names a real feeling, not one that invents an artificial obligation. "Some afternoons are made of gold" works because anyone who has painted an afternoon already knows the line is true. The line confirms an existing feeling. It does not manufacture one out of nothing.
