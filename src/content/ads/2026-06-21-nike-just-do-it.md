template: |
  1. Locate the exact gap where your audience's wanting dies. The hesitation right before action, not the desire itself.
  2. Compress the message into a verb-led imperative shorter than the hesitation. Three words is the magic number. The hesitation cannot get in edgewise.
  3. Leave the object of the verb undefined. The reader fills the it with their own private project. The brand becomes the universal solvent for hesitation.
  4. Pair the line with images of the action being done, not the product being used. The product is invisible.

  **A. Different category: a meditation app for distracted adults.**

  The category-default is to sell duration, calm, science, cortisol claims. The Just Do It move is to skip all of it.

  "Sit down."

  Two words, on a black screen, against the sound of someone breathing in. The app name appears, quietly, at the bottom of the screen on the second breath. No promise of inner peace. No countdown timer. Just the verb that the audience has been postponing for six months.

  **B. Same category, real brand: Nature's Shimmer Watercolor Workshop, $47.**

  The category-default for watercolor courses is to sell technique, beauty, the lessons themselves. The Just Do It move is to skip the deliberation about whether you are ready.

  "Open the paint."

  Three words, against a slow shot of a thumbnail prying the tin lid off a new pan of gold-flecked watercolor. The lid lifts. Light hits the pigment. The pigment hits the paper. The workshop name appears in italic serif in the corner only as the brush touches water for the first time.

  No price. No "beginners welcome." No "you'll learn." The opposite of the typical course ad, and exactly the move Nike made.

  The mechanism translates without strain. The hesitation Nike named (I should run) is the same hesitation Valerie's audience names (I should paint, I should make something, I should have started years ago). A note on what makes Just Do It survive thirty-eight years of category turnover: the verb is small enough to be carried in a pocket and large enough to mean anything. A teenager has used "just do it" to start a sentence with someone she has a crush on. A widow has used it to put on running shoes for the first time in a year. A founder has used it to file an LLC. Nike sold the line, not the shoes, and the line keeps being free.

The compression is doing the work. Most ads add explanation. This one removes it. Removal is rarer than addition and therefore more memorable. When the brain encounters a deliberate gap, it leans in to fill the gap, and what it fills it with becomes the brand's. That is also how mantras work.

What to steal: the discipline of compression. Before any draft, ask "what is the three-word version." If three words feels impossible, you have not yet located the audience's actual hesitation. Find the hesitation. Then write shorter than it.

What to leave behind: the celebrity scale. Nike has spent thirty-eight years stacking athletes behind the verb. A small brand does not need that. Walt Stack on a bridge at dawn was 80 years old and an unknown. That is the real lesson of the launch ad. The verb does not need a celebrity. It needs a person.
