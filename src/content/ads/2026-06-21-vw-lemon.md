template: |
  1. Find the most damning small thing about your product that 99% of customers would never notice.
  2. Lead the ad with that exact word or fact, in larger type than anything else on the page.
  3. Make the photograph or visual look UNDERWHELMING. No glamour. No drama. Mundane.
  4. Body copy: name a real person inside the company (with their name) who catches the flaw. Be specific about what they catch. Specificity does the believability work.
  5. Trust the reader to do the math. The strength is left unstated. The unstated thing is what they remember.
appliedExample: |
  Two examples. One in a different category to prove the template generalizes. One in the actual brand it is about to be used for to prove it fits.

  **A. Different category: an indie skincare brand.**

  Most skincare ads claim "transformative." Most show before/after. The Lemon move is the opposite.

  A single photograph of a small dark-brown bottle on a plain shelf. Headline: *Slow.*

  Body copy: "This serum takes six to eight weeks to do anything visible. Our chemist, Mira Aguilar, will not ship a formula that promises faster results because that would require ingredients she will not put on her own face. Most of our customers stop using it in the first three weeks because they expect a one-week miracle. The ones who stick with it tell their friends in month two. We do not run discount promotions for this reason. The slow ones are the ones that actually work."

  Brand mark at the bottom, small. No model. No claim.

  **B. Same category, real brand: Nature's Shimmer Watercolor Workshop, $47.**

  Most watercolor course ads claim "you will learn to paint like a pro in 30 days." Most show finished masterpieces. The Lemon move is the opposite.

  A single photograph of a piece of cotton paper with one small experimental wash that looks muddy and uncertain. Headline below it: *Week one looks worse than this.*

  Body copy: "Watercolor is the only medium where you ruin a hundred pages before the hundred-and-first looks like what was in your head. We do not promise otherwise. The workshop is four weeks long. Most of week one is muddy. Week two starts to thin out. Week three is when the shimmer arrives, usually for the first time on a page you weren't trying very hard on, which is itself part of the lesson. By week four most students have ten ruined pages and three they will frame. We tell you this up front because the only people who succeed in watercolor are the ones who can keep going past a muddy week one."

  Italic serif at the bottom: "Nature's Shimmer Watercolor Workshop · $47."

  The mechanism is identical. By naming the failure mode upfront, the brand signals an obsessive understanding of the actual experience. The audience's relief at being told the truth becomes the brand's currency. Confession is the only marketing format that does not deplete with overuse.

A note on what makes Lemon survive sixty-seven years of category change: the format is not the era. The mechanism (pre-emptive concession, real names, specific defects) is not 1950s. It is permanent. Every era produces marketing that is too polished to believe. Lemon is the format that reads as honest in any era. As long as the average ad keeps inflating, the format keeps working.

The discipline is harder than it looks. A confession only works if it is actually a concession. Brands that try to fake the Lemon move (manufactured "vulnerable" copy that turns out to be a humblebrag) get caught immediately. The audience can tell. Real flaws have specific texture. Manufactured flaws do not.

What to steal: name your actual weakness, by name, with specificity, in the largest type on the page. Resist the urge to recover at the end of the body copy. The reader will recover for you.

What to leave behind: nothing, really. The format is one of the most transferable in the entire swipe file. Black-and-white print, color magazine, social static, classified notice. The architecture survives every medium because the mechanism is psychological, not visual.
